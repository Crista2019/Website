July 21st, 2016

Let it be known to the masses that Crista Falk compares to a record in two respects:

1.) Despite her best efforts, she occasionally repeats the same thing over and over again, never to truly accomplish what she preaches.

2.) She gradually faded from the public eye, only to be revived after a considerable amount of time.

That being said, I have decided earnestly not to lie to myself. I will only write ever so often after significant events or after months of Internet silence, whichever comes first. I'm a programmer, darnit, not an author. The deception ends now.

Regardless, I feel I owe an explanation. Since I have not one to give, I'll instead write a recapitulation of my summer thus far. I graduated from the 9th grade without much cause for event. Subsequently, I was fortunate enough to then go on another college tour, this time of WSU and the University of Idaho. Both were fun and educational. I won a tee shirt from the former school in a peculiar way. I answered that the college was founded in 1890 correctly, simply because I read it on a sign somewhere on campus an hour earlier. Later that day I arbitrarily, yet correctly, guessed the answer to another question. I say vaguely that it had to do with sports, and that my answer was the year 2003. I'm not confident that I knew in the moment what was being asked, but I'm thoroughly convinced of my own psychic abilities. Sometime after that, I attended a week long camp about java programming and cyber security principles. It was unsuprisingly amazing, beneficial, and totally information-packed. A great deal of coding was done in Processing, and at the end of the camp, I had made over half a dozen executable sketches, some event-based and interactive. This summer, because of faculty changes, I haven't been able to do nearly as much volunteering at the library. I'm excited still to help out again at this year's Coeur d' Con, a comic book and pop culture convention held at the Coeur d' Alene Library. Last year was full of fun, in spite of the 8 hour shift I willingly committed to. Speaking (or rather, writing) of literature, I've been able to get some great reading in. My summer book synopsis includes the Princess Bride, Mort, Pride and Prejudice, a collection of Hawthorne short stories, and several pages of Don Quixote, which I just started as of this morning. Thinking about it in retrospect, I seemed to have chosen a variety of each genre: fantasy, science fiction, historical fiction, short stories, and satire. Maybe it's just an attest to my indecision. Other than this, I've done some studying of classical music as well. For fun. I appreciate that Vivaldi wrote oh so many oboe concertos, and my favorite piece has to be his Four Seasons. However, as an overall composer, I admire Pyotr Ilyich Tchaikovsky. Not only is his name one of the most fun to say out loud (PYO-tur ihl-YIHCH chy-KAWF-skee), but he's created some pretty influential stuff as well, I suppooose. Also, Bach jokes are far too mainstream. Much in the way that conclusions to written passages are overdone today in modern writing. I've never been one to conform.

Making nihilism a popular institution, just to be ironic,

April 17th, 2016

I have recently thought about my future in terms of college and career goals. I just attended an impressive trip around the Northwest and Seattle area, touring colleges with members of my highschool ETS group. I figure if I can span my anxiety and excitement about my future over the next four years, I will be able to divide it into more palatable segments over time. Logically, I should start considering my plans as soon as possible. That way I will be totally prepared. You know, because unforeseen circumstances never arise and priorities never ever change...

The dream job for me has always been to become a computer programmer or software engineer at a company that works to benefit the world around them. The work doesn't have to be immediately rewarding, and the company doesn't have to be an empire. My biggest hope is that my effort will be for a significant cause. I legitimately want to make the world a better place, as cliche as it may sound.

Biology and computer science are my two favorite branches of science, so where these two subjects converge, I want to be. With news of a novel programming language that can compile written code and upload it into living organisms allowing them to perform new functions, this is not such a far-fetched aspiration. Thanks, MIT. Computer science and molecular biology go together like peanut butter and jelly. Or peanut butter and chocolate. Or really just plain peanut butter. In this analogy, computer science is peanut butter. It comes in different variations and can be applied to any other branch to improve or change it. Maybe I should eat lunch before I write. At any rate, this is my present strategy, documented for my later self to look over and cringe, I'm sure.

Planning for the future, one sandwich metaphor at a time,

December 16th, 2015

At this point, I have begun beating a dead horse with the "I swear I'll write more often" thing. Even when it is solely for my own sake and not for the sake of my GIGANTIC fan-base, I seem to be unable to follow through.

On a more positive note, STAR WARS... ahhh!

Ok. SO, I've started writing code using a bit of C++, and I made this interesting library of morse code in which functions representing letters or phrases could be called and sent to either of two ports that connect to two different colored lights on an Arduino board. The phrase functions were comprised of functions that were letters, which in turn were comprised of a combination of da and or dit functions. These base functions flashed a light for a duration of either 1 second or 500 milliseconds, in a sense that were either long or short, with delays between and afterward in order for the message to come across clearly. Essentially, the two lights were able to communicate and carry on a conversation, even if one were not adept in morse code. The entire project took nearly three hours, and at the end I was forced to erase all traces of it and exit because I did not technically own the circuit board nor the computer I was typing on. I had my own sitting right across my room too. That's life, man. It's one big deleted program while an Arduino board mocks you for your lack of foresight. I suppose I'm just the Prometheus of writing code and trying buying Star Wars tickets. I forget until the latest possible second to think about what I should do.

But STAR WARS... ahhhhHHHHHHH!

Well, see you later Dark Side, my old friend,
